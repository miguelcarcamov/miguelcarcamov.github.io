<h5 class="research-area-subtitle">The topic</h5>
<p>Magnetic fields are everywhere in space, even where we cannot see them directly. Earth’s field turns a compass needle; the Sun’s field drives flares and the solar wind; on larger scales, galaxies and the gas between them are threaded by fields that influence how stars form and how energy is transported.</p>
<p>The open questions are ambitious: how strong are those fields, how are they arranged across the Milky Way and beyond, and do they help or hold back the birth of stars? Polarised radio light is one of our best witnesses—light that has been nudged by magnetised gas along the way, leaving a trace we can decode. With the SKA, that trace will be readable on a scale we have never had before.</p>

<h5 class="research-area-subtitle">What I do</h5>
<p>I develop methods to recover magnetic-field structure from polarised radio data, using techniques such as Faraday tomography and modern computing so that large surveys remain practical. The goal is reliable, reproducible tools that scientists can use on SKA-era data volumes.</p>
